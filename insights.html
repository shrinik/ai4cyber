Key Insights and Intelligence

This section discusses key insights and intelligence obtained using the AI4Cyber platform

Attack Surface

Key Insights

  • We found that scanning Shodan for medical devices by manufacturers such as Omron yields thousands of devices that were accessible on the public internet.
  • As hospitals focus on patient care through rapid developing technology, cybersecurity is not taken in consideration.
  • Due to the largely unmanaged or undermanaged state of many IoMT device assets on the hospital premise, it is implied that the standard approach for cyber security will be insufficient for hospital networks.
  • Although hospital networks are generally open for communication, it is vital to at least take steps to partially segment networks and use AI to scan for unauthorized access.

IoMT

Key Insights

  • We found that the top vulnerabilities for a hack often came from IoMT devices. These devices are often publicly accessible and can be found during the internet search. Hospitals are in the business of saving lives, as such, the IoMT devices are developed at a rapid pace without Cybersecurity being maintained.
  • In one single search of a manufacturer such as Omron, we found that NVD for CPE appeared 42 times in the dataset.
  • Simply, in order to save lives, technology must adapt quickly, devices are going to innately be unsecured regardless of vendor. Faulty programming, bugs in software and manufacturer backdoors lead to high risk devices.
  • The implication is that any one of the thousands of IoMT devices within a hospital can be a gateway to a hackerâ€™s entry into the network. Once in the network, a hacker can easily shut down life saving medical tools in demand for ransom and data loss may occur. The net result, people will die.
  • Wherever possible, these IoMT devices should be placed behind a strict Firewall or NAT and accessed with up-to-date protocols such as WPA3 and current standards of encryption to reduce the attack surface. Devices must maintain a ridged update schedule to immediately patch any vulnerabilities discovered by the manufacturer.

External Servers and Phishing

Key Insights

  • We found that the top type of attack in the hospital industry was Phishing. Healthcare employees receive an average of 14 to 59 malicious emails per year.
  • Unfortunately, due to antiquated email protocols that are still used today, blocking phishing emails is no easy task; a hacker simply changes emails or spoofs their email address.
  • While we expect demand for external servers such as websites and general internet access, the hospital industry can reduce their attack surface by providing better centralized cybersecurity services that proactively monitor emails through the use of AI and ML and clustering techniques to determine if a website is phishing or not.
  • The diamond models highlighted that phishing was a main point of concern and that hospitals are extremely lucrative due to their inability to cease operations. This suggests that the use of AI techniques need to be more widespread on the hospital networks so the hospital can proactively safe guard its assets.
  • Hospitals must maintain an open communication structure but AI can help maintain the openness while simultaneously providing strong cybersecurity support.
